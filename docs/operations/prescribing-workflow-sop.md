# Prescribing Workflow SOP

**Purpose:** Document the paper prescription process for day-1 clinic operations. Hollis Health is not using electronic prescribing (e-prescribing) at launch. This SOP governs how prescriptions are issued, logged, and communicated to pharmacies until e-prescribing is enabled.

## 1. Legal Framework and Scope

### 1.1 What the Membership Agreement says about prescriptions

The Membership Agreement explicitly excludes prescriptions from membership services. Per **Section 5.4**:

> "The following are not included in Membership fees unless expressly listed in Exhibit A: medical diagnosis, medical treatment, prescriptions, insurance-billed clinical services, and any other service furnished by an independent third party that is not specifically identified as included."

**Section 2.3** further clarifies that clinical services furnished by independent licensed physicians are billed separately and are not part of the membership fee.

**Implication:** Prescriptions Isaac writes as a licensed clinician in his clinical capacity are distinct from the wellness membership services. This distinction must be maintained in patient communications and records.

### 1.2 Disclaimer to include with every prescription encounter

Add this statement verbatim to the clinical note for any encounter that includes a prescription:

> "Prescription services provided during this visit are clinical services rendered by Isaac D. Landes in his capacity as a licensed clinician. These services are separate from and not included in the patient's Hollis Health wellness membership. Hollis Health LLC is not responsible for pharmacy fulfillment, drug interactions with medications prescribed outside this practice, or third-party pharmacy fees."

DO NOT omit this disclaimer from the chart note.

### 1.3 Controlled substances

Until you have confirmed DEA registration and a compliant paper Rx protocol for your state, **do not prescribe Schedule II–V controlled substances.** If a patient requires a controlled substance, refer to their primary care provider or an appropriate specialist. Document the referral in the chart.

## 2. Paper Prescription Process

### 2.1 Required supplies
- Pre-printed Rx pads with your name, NPI, DEA number (when applicable), clinic address, and phone number.
- Keep pads in a locked drawer when not in use. DO NOT leave blank Rx pads unsecured.

### 2.2 Writing the prescription
1. Verify patient identity and date of birth before writing.
2. Write legibly in ink. Include:
   - Patient full name and date of birth
   - Date prescribed
   - Drug name (generic preferred), strength, dosage form
   - Sig (directions for use)
   - Quantity (written out and in numerals, e.g., "thirty (30)")
   - Number of refills (0 if none)
   - Your signature, printed name, NPI, and phone number
3. For each Rx written, note in the clinical chart: drug name, dose, quantity, directions, and reason for prescribing.

### 2.3 Patient instructions
- Hand the paper Rx directly to the patient. DO NOT fax unless the patient specifically requests pharmacy fax and you have confirmed the pharmacy fax number directly.
- Tell the patient which pharmacies are likely to have the medication in stock if you know.
- Instruct the patient to call the clinic if the pharmacy has questions: (210) 891-9005.

### 2.4 Logging
- Record in the **Rx Log Binder** (kept in the clinic): date, patient name (last, first), DOB, drug, dose, quantity, refills.
- The Rx Log Binder is a controlled document. Keep it locked with the Rx pads.
- DO NOT log controlled substance prescriptions in the same binder as non-controlled — keep a separate DEA-required log when applicable.

## 3. Pharmacy Communications

### 3.1 Pharmacy calls for verification
Pharmacies will call to verify prescriptions. When a pharmacy calls:
1. Confirm the caller's name and pharmacy name.
2. Ask for the patient name and medication they are calling about.
3. Confirm or deny the prescription as appropriate.
4. Log the call in the patient chart: "Pharmacy verification call — [pharmacy name] — [date] — [outcome]."

### 3.2 Pharmacy requests for additional information
If a pharmacy requests a change (e.g., insurance requires a different dosage form):
1. Assess whether the change is clinically appropriate.
2. If yes: call the pharmacy back and verbally authorize the change. Document in the chart.
3. If no: decline and explain why. Document in the chart.

DO NOT authorize changes to controlled substance prescriptions verbally without also sending a corrected written Rx.

## 4. Refill Requests

### 4.1 Standard refills
- Patients request refills via secure messaging in the app or by phone.
- Review the chart before authorizing any refill. Confirm the patient has been seen within a clinically appropriate timeframe for the medication.
- For chronic medications: if the patient has not been seen in more than 90 days, schedule a follow-up visit before refilling (unless clinical judgment supports an exception).

### 4.2 Refill process
1. Write a new paper Rx (same process as §2.2).
2. Mail or have the patient pick up the paper Rx, or call it in verbally to the pharmacy (where permitted for non-controlled substances by your state).
3. Document the refill in the patient chart.

## 5. Transition to E-Prescribing

When e-prescribing is implemented:
- This SOP is superseded by the e-prescribing platform's own workflow documentation.
- Any active paper Rx logs must be archived (do not destroy — retain per your state's medical records retention requirements, minimum 7 years for Texas).
- Update this doc's header to mark it as archived and link to the new SOP.
